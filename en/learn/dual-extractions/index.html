Dual Extraction of Medicinal Mushrooms

Medicinal mushrooms contain compounds with different chemical properties and solubility. Dual extraction — using both alcohol and hot water — allows a broader spectrum of compounds to be obtained from the mushroom.

Author: Rok Golob (GoMushroom)

Medicinal mushrooms contain a complex mixture of bioactive compounds. These compounds differ significantly in their chemical properties and solubility, which means that no single solvent can efficiently extract all of them.

For this reason, extraction methods play an important role in determining the composition and quality of the final mushroom extract.

Two major groups of compounds are commonly discussed in medicinal mushrooms:

  • Polysaccharides (including beta-glucans)
  • Terpenoids such as triterpenes

Because these groups differ in their chemical properties, different extraction approaches are required to obtain them effectively.

The Structure of the Mushroom Cell Wall

One of the reasons extraction is necessary lies in the structure of the fungal cell wall. Mushroom cell walls are composed of a complex structural matrix that includes:

  • chitin
  • beta-glucans
  • structural proteins

This structure gives fungal cells considerable mechanical strength. Many bioactive compounds remain embedded within this matrix and are therefore not easily released without proper processing.

Extraction helps disrupt this structure and allows compounds to dissolve into a liquid phase.

Water-Soluble Compounds: Polysaccharides and Beta-Glucans

Among the most widely studied compounds in medicinal mushrooms are polysaccharides, particularly beta-glucans.

Beta-glucans found in fungi typically consist of glucose molecules linked through a backbone of β-(1→3) glycosidic bonds with additional β-(1→6) branching.

This structure differs from beta-glucans found in cereals such as oats or barley and is characteristic for fungal cell walls.

Beta-glucans are primarily obtained through hot water extraction, which helps break down the fungal matrix and release these polysaccharides into solution.

Historically, mushrooms were often consumed as decoctions or teas, which represent a traditional form of water extraction.

Alcohol-Soluble Compounds: Triterpenes

Some compounds present in medicinal mushrooms are poorly soluble in water but dissolve well in alcohol.

A well-known example is Reishi (Ganoderma lucidum), which contains a group of triterpenoids known as ganoderic acids.

These molecules contribute to the characteristic bitter taste of Reishi and are typically extracted using alcohol.

Because of their chemical structure, they are not efficiently extracted through water extraction alone.

How Dual Extraction Works

Since medicinal mushrooms contain both water-soluble and alcohol-soluble compounds, many producers use a method known as dual extraction.

In this approach two different solvents are used sequentially:

  1. Alcohol extraction for alcohol-soluble compounds
  2. Hot water extraction for polysaccharides and beta-glucans
  3. Combination of both fractions

This process aims to obtain a broader spectrum of compounds naturally present in the mushroom.

Concentration of Mushroom Extracts

After extraction, the resulting liquid extracts are often concentrated in order to increase the amount of extracted compounds per unit volume.

Concentration is commonly performed under reduced pressure, allowing solvents to evaporate at lower temperatures and helping preserve temperature-sensitive compounds.

Interpreting Beta-Glucan Content

Beta-glucans are frequently used as an indicator of the polysaccharide fraction in mushroom products.

However, beta-glucan values alone do not necessarily describe the overall quality of a mushroom extract.

Beta-glucans are structural components of fungal cell walls and may also be present in powdered mushrooms that have not undergone extraction.

For this reason, extraction methods play an important role in determining how accessible these compounds are in the final extract.

Conclusion

Medicinal mushrooms contain compounds with different chemical properties and solubility.

Dual extraction combines the strengths of both water and alcohol extraction, allowing a broader range of naturally occurring compounds to be obtained from the mushroom.

Understanding these processes helps explain why mushroom products on the market can vary significantly in composition and quality.
