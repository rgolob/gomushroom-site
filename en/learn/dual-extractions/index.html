Dual Extraction of Medicinal Mushrooms

Why both alcohol and hot water extraction are used to obtain a broader spectrum of compounds from medicinal mushrooms.

Author: Rok Golob Topic: Extraction methods

Introduction

Medicinal mushrooms contain a complex mixture of bioactive compounds. These compounds differ significantly in their chemical properties and solubility, which means that no single solvent can efficiently extract all of them.

For this reason, extraction methods play an important role in determining the composition and quality of the final mushroom extract.

Two major groups of compounds are commonly discussed in medicinal mushrooms: polysaccharides (including beta-glucans) and terpenoids such as triterpenes.

A key principle in extraction chemistry is the concept that “similar dissolves similar”. In practice this means that the polarity of the solvent strongly influences which molecules can dissolve in it. Water is a highly polar solvent and therefore efficiently extracts polar molecules such as polysaccharides and other carbohydrate-based compounds. Alcohols such as ethanol are less polar and are therefore more suitable for extracting less polar compounds such as many terpenoids.

Because of this difference, extraction with pure water or pure alcohol alone rarely provides an optimal result. Alcoholic extractions used for medicinal mushrooms are therefore usually performed with ethanol-water mixtures. The exact ethanol concentration can vary depending on the compounds of interest. For example, triterpenes found in Ganoderma lucidum are typically extracted using relatively high ethanol concentrations, while other compounds reported in mushrooms, such as cordycepin from Cordyceps species, can be extracted efficiently at considerably lower alcohol concentrations.

In contrast, when the goal is to extract polysaccharides such as beta-glucans, water becomes the preferred solvent. In fact, the presence of significant amounts of alcohol can even reduce the efficiency of polysaccharide extraction because these molecules are poorly soluble in ethanol-rich mixtures.

For this reason many mushroom extraction processes combine different solvents in separate stages, allowing each group of compounds to be extracted under conditions where it is most soluble.

The Structure of the Mushroom Cell Wall

One of the main reasons extraction is required lies in the structure of the fungal cell wall. Unlike plant cells, which contain cellulose as their primary structural component, fungal cell walls are composed of a complex matrix of chitin, beta-glucans and structural proteins.

Chitin is a long-chain polymer of N-acetylglucosamine and forms a rigid structural scaffold that provides mechanical strength to fungal tissues. Embedded within this framework are various glucans, particularly β-(1→3) and β-(1→6) glucans, which form a three-dimensional network throughout the cell wall.

These beta-glucans are among the most studied bioactive polysaccharides in medicinal mushrooms. However, within the intact fungal cell wall they are tightly associated with chitin and other structural components, forming a matrix that is not easily disrupted.

Because of this structural complexity, many of these molecules remain trapped inside the fungal cell wall and are not readily released without processing. Mechanical grinding alone is often insufficient to fully liberate them.

Thermal processing and hot water extraction help break down this structural matrix, allowing polysaccharides to dissolve into the surrounding liquid. This is one of the reasons why many traditional preparations of medicinal mushrooms historically involved long boiling or decoction processes.

Modern extraction techniques build on this principle by applying controlled temperature, solvent composition and processing conditions to improve the release of bioactive compounds from the fungal matrix.

Solvent Polarity and Extraction

A useful way to understand mushroom extraction is through solvent polarity. In general, polar solvents dissolve polar compounds more efficiently, while less polar solvents are better suited for less polar molecules.

This is why water is commonly used for polysaccharides such as beta-glucans, while ethanol-based mixtures are used for less polar compounds such as terpenoids, phenolic compounds and other lipophilic metabolites.

Simplified view of solvent polarity in mushroom extraction: water is highly polar, ethanol-water mixtures provide intermediate polarity, and less polar solvent environments are better suited for lipophilic compounds.

Water-Soluble Compounds: Polysaccharides and Beta-Glucans

Among the most widely studied compounds in medicinal mushrooms are polysaccharides, particularly beta-glucans. These molecules are long chains of glucose units linked together through specific glycosidic bonds.

In fungi, the most characteristic structure consists of a backbone of β-(1→3) linked glucose units with additional β-(1→6) branches. This branching structure creates complex three-dimensional molecules that are typical for fungal polysaccharides.

It is important to distinguish these beta-glucans from alpha-glucans. Alpha-glucans are also polymers of glucose, but their molecular structure differs because the glucose units are connected through α-type glycosidic bonds. These structures are commonly found in storage carbohydrates such as starch and glycogen.

Because of these structural differences, beta-glucans and alpha-glucans behave differently chemically and biologically. For this reason analytical methods used for mushroom extracts often distinguish between total glucans, beta-glucans and alpha-glucans.

Beta-glucans in mushrooms are primarily extracted using hot water extraction. Their relatively high polarity makes them readily soluble in water once the fungal cell wall structure has been sufficiently disrupted through processing.

However, the presence of beta-glucans alone does not automatically describe the overall composition or quality of a mushroom extract. Mushrooms naturally contain many other classes of compounds, including terpenoids, sterols, phenolic compounds and small metabolites that may require different extraction conditions.

For this reason, extraction methods are often designed to obtain different groups of compounds under conditions where they are most soluble.

Alcohol-Soluble Compounds: Terpenoids and Other Lipophilic Molecules

While many polysaccharides dissolve well in water, medicinal mushrooms also contain a wide range of compounds that are more soluble in alcohol. These compounds are typically less polar and therefore dissolve more efficiently in ethanol-based solvent mixtures.

One of the best known examples are the triterpenoids found in Ganoderma lucidum (Reishi). These molecules belong mainly to the lanostane-type triterpenes, including compounds known as ganoderic acids. They contribute to the characteristic bitter taste of Reishi extracts and are typically obtained through alcoholic extraction.

Other medicinal mushrooms contain additional classes of alcohol-soluble compounds. For example, Hericium erinaceus (Lion’s Mane) contains various diterpenoid compounds, including molecules commonly referred to as hericenones, which are typically extracted more efficiently using alcohol-containing solvents.

In the case of Inonotus obliquus (Chaga), alcohol extraction may also recover compounds derived from the host tree, particularly betulin and betulinic acid, which originate from the birch bark on which the fungus grows. These triterpenoid compounds are poorly soluble in water but dissolve readily in alcohol.

In addition to terpenoids, alcoholic extracts may also contain various phenolic compounds, sterols and other lipophilic metabolites that contribute to the overall chemical composition of mushroom extracts.

Because these molecules differ significantly from water-soluble polysaccharides in their polarity and chemical behavior, alcohol extraction is often used as a complementary step when a broader range of compounds is desired.

How Dual Extraction Works

Because medicinal mushrooms contain compounds with different chemical properties and solubility, a single extraction step rarely captures the full range of molecules present in the fungal material. For this reason, many extraction processes use a method commonly referred to as dual extraction.

Dual extraction is based on the principle of sequential solvent extraction, where different solvents are used in separate stages to dissolve compounds according to their polarity and chemical characteristics.

In simplified terms, two main extraction phases are typically involved. An alcohol-based extraction is used to dissolve less polar compounds such as terpenoids, sterols and various lipophilic metabolites. In a separate step, a hot water extraction is performed to extract polar compounds, particularly polysaccharides such as beta-glucans.

During these steps the mushroom material is exposed to different solvent environments, allowing each group of compounds to dissolve under conditions where it is most soluble. The resulting liquids can be viewed as separate extract fractions, each containing different types of molecules.

After the individual extraction stages are completed, the resulting fractions may be combined to produce a final extract that contains compounds obtained from both solvent systems.

The goal of this approach is not simply to increase extraction yield, but to obtain a broader representation of the naturally occurring compounds present in the mushroom.

Different producers may apply variations of this concept, adjusting parameters such as solvent composition, temperature, extraction time or concentration steps depending on the desired composition of the final extract.

Dual vs Triple Extraction

In discussions about mushroom extracts the terms dual extraction and triple extraction are sometimes used in slightly different ways depending on how the extraction process is described.

The term dual extraction is most commonly used when two different types of solvents are involved in the extraction process. In medicinal mushroom extraction this typically refers to the combination of alcohol extraction and hot water extraction, allowing both alcohol-soluble and water-soluble compounds to be obtained.

However, some producers use the term triple extraction to describe processes where the mushroom material undergoes three separate extraction stages. In this context the distinction refers not to the number of solvents used, but to the number of extraction steps applied during processing.

For example, an extraction process may involve an initial alcohol extraction followed by two water-based extraction stages performed under different conditions. These additional steps can help further disrupt the fungal cell wall and release compounds that may remain partially trapped within the structural matrix of the mushroom.

In some extraction approaches the later stages may involve higher temperatures or pressure-assisted conditions. Such conditions can help break down the rigid fungal cell wall composed of chitin and glucans, improving the release of certain polysaccharides into the extraction medium.

Because terminology in this area is not fully standardized, both expressions are sometimes used to describe similar concepts. Ultimately the effectiveness of an extract depends less on the name of the process and more on how well the extraction method releases the naturally occurring compounds present in the mushroom.

Interpreting Beta-Glucan Content

Beta-glucans are frequently used as an indicator of the polysaccharide fraction in mushroom products.

However, beta-glucan values alone do not necessarily describe the overall quality of a mushroom extract.

Beta-glucans are structural components of fungal cell walls and may also be present in powdered mushrooms that have not undergone extraction.

For this reason, extraction methods play an important role in determining how accessible these compounds are in the final extract.

Conclusion

Medicinal mushrooms contain compounds with different chemical properties and solubility.

Dual extraction combines the strengths of both water and alcohol extraction, allowing a broader range of naturally occurring compounds to be obtained from the mushroom.

Understanding these processes helps explain why mushroom products on the market can vary significantly in composition and quality.

Key Takeaways

  • Medicinal mushrooms contain many different classes of compounds, including polysaccharides, terpenoids, phenolic compounds and other metabolites.
  • Because these molecules differ in polarity and chemical structure, they dissolve best in different types of solvents.
  • Water extraction is typically used to obtain polar compounds such as beta-glucans, while alcohol extraction can dissolve less polar molecules such as terpenoids and other lipophilic compounds.
  • Dual extraction combines these approaches in separate stages, allowing a broader range of compounds to be obtained from the mushroom material.
  • The final extract therefore represents a combination of different extraction fractions, each obtained under conditions where specific groups of compounds are most soluble.
