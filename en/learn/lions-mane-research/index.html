What Does Research Say About Lion's Mane Mushroom?

Lion's Mane (Hericium erinaceus) is one of the most studied medicinal mushrooms for nervous system research. An overview of what scientists are currently investigating — and what we actually know.

Author: Rok Golob Topic: Medicinal Mushrooms • Research • Lion's Mane

Introduction

Lion's Mane (Hericium erinaceus) is one of the most studied medicinal mushrooms in the field of neuroscience. Researchers are particularly interested in its bioactive compounds — hericenones, erinacines, polysaccharides and beta-glucans. The greatest attention has been drawn to a possible link with Nerve Growth Factor (NGF), neuroplasticity, nerve regeneration and cognitive function. [1]

It is important to note that a large part of the research is still based on laboratory models and animal studies. Clinical trials in humans are fewer, so findings cannot be directly equated with effects in individual people. [1]

What is Lion's Mane?

Lion's Mane (Hericium erinaceus) is an edible mushroom that grows primarily on hardwood trees. It is easily recognised by its distinctive white cascading spines — hence the common English name.

It contains several groups of bioactive compounds, of which the most studied are:

  • polysaccharides,
  • beta-glucans,
  • hericenones,
  • erinacines,
  • phenolic compounds.

Hericenones and erinacines in particular are one of the main reasons Lion's Mane has become so interesting to researchers studying the nervous system. [1]

Lion's Mane (Hericium erinaceus) is one of the most recognisable medicinal mushrooms, known for its unique white spines. The compounds hericenones and erinacines are the reason it has become the subject of numerous nervous system studies.

Why Are Hericenones and Erinacines Particularly Interesting?

Most medicinal mushrooms attract research interest primarily because of their polysaccharides and beta-glucans. Lion's Mane has these compounds too — but hericenones and erinacines are what set it apart from all others.

Hericenones are aromatic compounds found mainly in the fruiting body. Erinacines are diterpenoids found primarily in the mycelium. Both groups are of interest in laboratory research in the context of processes related to NGF and the nervous system. [1]

The difference between fruiting body and mycelium is therefore fundamental for Lion's Mane. They do not have the same chemical composition — and this influences which compounds are present in the extract. Most laboratory and clinical studies dealing with hericenones are based on fruiting body extracts. [1]

NGF and the nervous system

NGF (Nerve Growth Factor) is a signalling molecule involved in the growth, maintenance and regeneration of nerve cells.

Laboratory studies suggest that certain compounds from Lion's Mane — primarily hericenones and erinacines — may influence processes related to NGF synthesis and neurite outgrowth. [1]

NGF plays an important role in:

  • neuronal survival,
  • the formation of connections between nerve cells,
  • learning,
  • memory,
  • neuroplasticity.

These connections are precisely why Lion's Mane has become one of the most interesting mushrooms in nervous system research. [1]

Focus, concentration and mental clarity

When people talk about Lion's Mane, they often mention:

  • focus,
  • concentration,
  • mental clarity,
  • so-called brain fog.

In a randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled study in healthy young adults, researchers observed possible improvements in the speed of certain cognitive tasks and a reduction in subjective stress after taking a Lion's Mane preparation. The authors noted that the study was small and that larger studies are needed. [2]

It is therefore more accurate to say that the results are promising, not conclusive. Lion's Mane is not considered a stimulant, but rather a mushroom that researchers are exploring in the context of longer-term processes of nervous system function.

Nerve regeneration

One of the most intriguing areas of Lion's Mane research is nerve regeneration.

In an animal model study, researchers examined the effect of an aqueous extract of Lion's Mane on recovery following peripheral nerve injury. Results showed faster functional recovery compared to the control group. [3]

Such results are particularly interesting for the field of regenerative medicine, but cannot be directly translated to humans without additional clinical research.

Stomach and digestion

Beyond the nervous system, researchers are also interested in Lion's Mane effects on the digestive tract.

An animal model study showed that an aqueous extract of Lion's Mane protected the gastric mucosa from ethanol-induced injury. The effect observed in the study was dose-dependent. [4]

This supports interest in Lion's Mane for gastric mucosal protection and digestive health.

Researchers are also studying the effect of Lion's Mane polysaccharides on gut microbiota and the digestive system. Most of this research is still based on laboratory and animal models. [5]

Immune system

Although Lion's Mane is best known for its nervous system research, it also contains polysaccharides and beta-glucans studied for their influence on the immune system.

Laboratory and animal studies suggest that polysaccharides from Lion's Mane may influence immune responses, including via macrophages, cytokines and the gut immune environment. [5]

It is important to note that immune modulation is a more accurate term than immune stimulation — it is not simply a matter of "boosting" the immune system, but rather influencing its regulation. [5]

For Lion's Mane, this is not the primary research focus compared to reishi, but it is an important part of the broader picture of its bioactive properties.

Anti-inflammatory effects, oxidative stress and regenerative potential

Lion's Mane contains compounds that researchers are also studying for their anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties.

Review studies report the influence of various Lion's Mane compounds on oxidative stress, inflammatory signalling pathways and cell protection in laboratory and animal models. [1]

This area is significant because inflammation and oxidative stress often intersect with nervous system function, digestion, immune response and ageing processes.

Some review articles also mention the regenerative potential of Lion's Mane, particularly in connection with the nervous system and tissue repair processes. Current data are largely limited to laboratory and animal models, so definitive conclusions for human use cannot yet be drawn. [6]

What Does Research Show Well?

The most consistently studied areas for Lion's Mane are NGF and neuroplasticity, focus and cognitive function, nerve regeneration, gastric mucosal protection, immune modulation, and anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects.

The strongest evidence currently comes from laboratory and animal studies. The most notable findings are in the areas of NGF synthesis, neurite outgrowth, and gastric mucosal protection.

Where Is the Data Still Limited?

Clinical research in humans is more limited. Most human studies involve small groups and short timeframes. This means that while the direction of research is promising, firm conclusions about effects for any given individual cannot yet be drawn.

Areas such as tissue regeneration, anti-inflammatory effects and immune modulation are primarily documented in laboratory and animal models. Human clinical data in these areas is still scarce.
