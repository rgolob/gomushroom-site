EXTRACTION BASICS

What Is Extraction?

How valuable compounds move from raw material into liquid — and why the extraction method defines the final product.

By Rok Golob GoMushroom Updated: May 5, 2026

Extraction is the fundamental process behind every herbal extract, mushroom tincture or plant-based formulation. At its core, extraction is simple: it is the transfer of compounds from a solid material into a liquid solvent.

But in practice, the quality of an extract depends on how well this process is understood and controlled. The solvent, temperature, time, particle size and structure of the raw material all influence what ends up in the final extract.

Extraction is a transfer process

A dried mushroom or plant contains many different compounds locked inside its structure. Extraction begins when a solvent is added. The solvent penetrates the material, dissolves specific compounds and carries them into solution.

This movement is driven by concentration gradients, diffusion, temperature and physical access to the material. The better these factors are controlled, the more efficient and consistent the extraction becomes.

Not all compounds dissolve the same way

One of the most important principles in extraction is: like dissolves like. Different compounds require different solvents.

Water extraction

Water is a polar solvent. It is suitable for extracting more water-soluble compounds such as polysaccharides, including beta-glucans, proteins and certain minerals.

Ethanol extraction

Ethanol is less polar than water. It helps extract compounds such as terpenes, triterpenes, phenolic compounds, resins and other less water-soluble molecules.

This is why a single extraction method is rarely sufficient. If only water is used, many alcohol-soluble compounds may remain in the raw material. If only ethanol is used, important water-soluble compounds may be left behind.

The material itself can be a barrier

Extraction is not only about chemistry. It is also about structure. In medicinal mushrooms, valuable compounds are held inside a dense matrix made of chitin, beta-glucans and proteins.

This structure can limit how easily a solvent enters the material. Simple soaking may therefore be slow or incomplete, especially when the raw material is coarse, compact or poorly wetted.

What improves extraction efficiency?

Efficient extraction requires both chemical and physical control. Several factors influence how well compounds move from the raw material into the solvent.

Particle size

Grinding increases surface area and improves contact between the raw material and the solvent.

Temperature

Higher temperatures can increase solubility and diffusion rates, especially during water extraction.

Time

Longer extraction allows more compounds to migrate into the solvent, although every process eventually reaches a practical limit.

Solvent ratio

Larger solvent volumes can improve extraction efficiency, but they also create a more dilute extract that later needs to be concentrated.

Mechanical assistance

Techniques such as ultrasonic extraction can help disrupt material structure, improve solvent penetration and accelerate mass transfer.

Extraction is only half of the process

Efficient extraction often requires large volumes of solvent. But the final product should be concentrated, stable and usable.

This is why extraction is usually followed by pressing, filtration and concentration. Without concentration, many extracts would remain too dilute for practical use.

From extraction to formulation

A high-quality extract is not defined by one step alone. It depends on the entire process:

  1. Selection of raw material
  2. Choice of extraction solvent
  3. Extraction temperature and time
  4. Separation and filtration
  5. Vacuum concentration
  6. Final formulation

Each step affects the final composition. Two products made from the same raw material can be very different if the extraction process is different.

Why this matters

Extraction determines which compounds are present, in what concentration and in what balance. It is not just a technical step. It is the foundation of the final product.

This is especially important in medicinal mushrooms, where different compound groups require different extraction conditions. A well-designed process can capture a broader spectrum of compounds than a single simple extraction.

FAQ

What is extraction in simple terms?

Extraction is the process of transferring compounds from a solid material into a liquid solvent.

Why are different solvents used?

Different compounds dissolve in different solvents. Water extracts more polar compounds, while ethanol helps extract less polar compounds.

Is one extraction method enough?

Often not. Many natural materials contain different compound groups that require different extraction conditions.

Why are mushroom extracts difficult to produce?

Medicinal mushrooms have a dense structural matrix made of chitin, beta-glucans and proteins, which can limit solvent penetration.
