What Is Extraction? How Compounds Move From Raw Material Into Liquid

Extraction is the process that defines every mushroom tincture and plant extract. It determines which compounds are present, in what concentration, and in what balance.

What Is Extraction

Extraction is the process of transferring compounds from a solid material into a liquid solvent.

At first glance, extraction may appear simple: immerse the material in a solvent and obtain an extract.

In practice, extraction is significantly more complex. Natural materials such as medicinal mushrooms contain multiple classes of compounds with different chemical properties, polarity and solubility.

As a result, extraction is not just about soaking material in liquid, but about controlling how efficiently different compounds are released and dissolved.

The final composition of an extract depends on solvent selection, temperature, extraction time and the structure of the raw material itself.

Extraction Is a Transfer Process

A dried mushroom or plant contains compounds locked inside its structure.

When a solvent is introduced, it penetrates the material, dissolves specific compounds and carries them into solution.

This process is driven by diffusion, concentration gradients and physical interaction between the solvent and the material.

The more efficiently these factors are controlled, the more consistent and complete the extraction becomes.

Not All Compounds Dissolve the Same Way

A fundamental principle of extraction is that compounds dissolve best in solvents with similar chemical properties.

Water, being highly polar, efficiently extracts polysaccharides, including beta-glucans, as well as proteins and some minerals.

Ethanol, with lower polarity, is more suitable for extracting triterpenes, phenolics and other less water-soluble compounds.

Using only one solvent results in only a partial extraction of the available compounds.

This is why single-phase extraction methods always represent a limited use of the raw material.

The Structure of the Material Matters

Extraction is not only limited by chemistry, but also by the physical structure of the material.

In medicinal mushrooms, compounds are embedded within a dense matrix composed of chitin, beta-glucans and proteins.

This structure acts as a barrier, limiting how easily a solvent can penetrate and access the compounds inside.

As a result, simple soaking is often inefficient and requires more time, more solvent or additional processing methods.

What Improves Extraction Efficiency

Efficient extraction requires both chemical and physical control of the process.

Several factors influence how well compounds move from the raw material into the solvent.

Particle size: Smaller particles increase surface area and improve contact with the solvent.

Temperature: Higher temperatures increase solubility and diffusion rates.

Time: Longer extraction allows more compounds to migrate into the solvent.

Solvent ratio: Larger solvent volumes improve extraction efficiency but require later concentration.

Mechanical assistance: Methods such as ultrasonic extraction can improve penetration and accelerate the process.

Extraction Is Only Half of the Process

Efficient extraction often requires large volumes of solvent.

However, the final product should be concentrated, stable and practical to use.

This is why extraction is followed by pressing, filtration and concentration.

Without concentration, extracts would remain too dilute.

Why This Matters

Extraction determines which compounds are present, in what concentration and in what balance.

Two products made from the same raw material can be completely different depending on how they are extracted.

Extraction is therefore not just a technical step, but one of the key factors that defines the final product.
